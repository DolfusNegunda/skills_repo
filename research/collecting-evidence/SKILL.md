# Collecting Evidence

## Scope
The disciplined gathering of evidence: planning what to look for, searching
systematically, capturing each source with full provenance, and organizing findings
for traceable analysis. The front end of any research effort. Judging source quality
is [assessing-source-credibility](../assessing-source-credibility/SKILL.md).

## Purpose
Build an evidence base a conclusion can stand on — broad enough to avoid bias,
captured with enough provenance to verify and cite, and organized so anyone can trace
a claim back to its source.

## When to use this skill
- "Gather evidence / sources / research this question / find support for X."
- Building the evidence base before analysis or synthesis.
- Any task where conclusions must be traceable and reproducible.

## When NOT to use this skill
- Judging source quality → [assessing-source-credibility](../assessing-source-credibility/SKILL.md).
- Confirming a specific claim → [verifying-facts](../verifying-facts/SKILL.md).
- Combining findings into an answer → [synthesizing-research](../synthesizing-research/SKILL.md).

## Inputs
- The research question or claim, and the required rigor/scope.
- Constraints: time, access (paywalls, internal data), and recency needs.

## Outputs
- A structured evidence base: each item with source, provenance (author, date, URL/
  DOI), the relevant finding/quote, and a note on relevance — organized for synthesis.

## Workflow
```
Progress:
- [ ] 1. Frame the question and what would count as evidence
- [ ] 2. Plan searches: terms, sources, and diversity of angle
- [ ] 3. Search systematically; capture full provenance as you go
- [ ] 4. Record the finding + location for each source
- [ ] 5. Seek disconfirming evidence deliberately
- [ ] 6. Organize for synthesis; note gaps
```

**Step 1 — define what counts as evidence** for this question (data? peer-reviewed
studies? primary documents?) so you gather signal, not noise. **Step 3 — capture
provenance at collection time:** author, date, publisher, URL/DOI, and the exact
location of the finding. Recovering this later is painful and often impossible.
**Step 5 — hunt disconfirming evidence on purpose.** Gathering only supporting
sources is cherry-picking; a sound base includes what challenges the hypothesis.

## Principles
1. **Provenance at capture** — author, date, source, location, every time.
2. **Breadth and diversity** — multiple angles and source types.
3. **Seek disconfirmation,** not just support.
4. **Traceable** — every future claim maps back to a captured source.
5. **Note gaps** — what you couldn't find is itself a finding.

## Decision framework
- **High-stakes/formal?** Systematic search with documented terms and inclusion criteria.
- **Quick scan?** Still capture provenance and seek at least one counter-source.
- **Conflicting evidence?** Capture both sides; resolution is for synthesis.
- **Behind paywalls?** Note the gap and access limits; don't silently omit.

## Common mistakes
- **No provenance** — links with no date/author, unciteable and unverifiable.
- **Cherry-picking** — collecting only confirming evidence.
- **Single-source-type bias** — all blogs, or all one journal.
- **No search plan** — ad hoc googling, unreproducible.
- **Losing the finding location** — can't point to what supported the claim.

## Validation checklist
- [ ] The question and evidence criteria are defined.
- [ ] Searches planned and diverse in angle/source type.
- [ ] Every source captured with full provenance.
- [ ] The relevant finding + its location recorded per source.
- [ ] Disconfirming evidence actively sought.
- [ ] Base organized for synthesis; gaps noted.

## Edge cases
- **Fast-moving topics:** record access dates; findings may age quickly.
- **Grey literature/primary data:** capture context and limitations, not just the number.
- **Internal/confidential sources:** note access constraints and handling rules.
- **Contradictory evidence:** keep it; don't resolve prematurely by dropping the inconvenient source.

## Examples
**Input:** "Gather evidence on whether four-day work weeks improve productivity."
**Output:** A search plan (terms, source types: trials, meta-analyses, company case
studies, critiques); an evidence table capturing each study's author/date/DOI, the
productivity finding, sample/context, and relevance; deliberately including null and
negative results; gaps noted (few long-term studies) — ready for credibility
assessment and synthesis.

- Maintain an evidence table (source | provenance | finding | relevance) as you collect.
