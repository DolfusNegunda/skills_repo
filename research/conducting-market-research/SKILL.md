# Conducting Market Research

## Scope
Understanding a market to inform decisions: sizing (TAM/SAM/SOM), segmentation,
customer needs and demand, and trends. Feeds go-to-market, product, and investment
decisions. Competitor-specific analysis is
[analyzing-competitors](../analyzing-competitors/SKILL.md).

## Purpose
Replace assumptions about "the market" with evidenced insight: how big it realistically
is, who the customers are and what they need, whether demand exists, and where it's
heading — with the uncertainty stated honestly.

## When to use this skill
- "Do market research / size this market / who are the customers?"
- "Is there demand for X / assess this market opportunity?"
- Informing go-to-market, product, pricing, or investment decisions.

## When NOT to use this skill
- Competitor landscape → [analyzing-competitors](../analyzing-competitors/SKILL.md).
- Technology fit → [evaluating-technology](../evaluating-technology/SKILL.md).
- The investment case → [writing-business-cases](../../business/writing-business-cases/SKILL.md).

## Inputs
- The market/product and the decision the research informs.
- Access to secondary sources (reports, data) and any primary channels (customers, surveys).

## Outputs
- Market insight: sizing (TAM/SAM/SOM with method shown), segments, needs/demand
  evidence, trends, and implications — each with sources and stated confidence.

## Workflow
```
Progress:
- [ ] 1. Define the market boundary and the decision it informs
- [ ] 2. Size it: TAM/SAM/SOM, showing the method and assumptions
- [ ] 3. Segment customers; profile needs per segment
- [ ] 4. Assess demand and willingness to pay
- [ ] 5. Read trends and drivers (what's changing, why)
- [ ] 6. Draw implications; state confidence and gaps
```

**Step 1 — define the market precisely.** "The AI market" is meaningless; "mid-market
HR teams needing automated onboarding in North America" is sizable. **Step 2 — show
the sizing method:** TAM (total), SAM (serviceable), SOM (realistically obtainable),
built top-down (from reports) and bottom-up (units × price), reconciled. A market size
with no method is a guess — always show the assumptions. **Step 4 — demand evidence:**
stated interest is weak; behavioral signals (spending, searches, adoption of
substitutes) are strong. **Step 6 — state confidence honestly;** market numbers carry
real uncertainty, and false precision misleads decisions.

## Principles
1. **Define the market narrowly** enough to be real.
2. **Show the sizing method** (top-down + bottom-up); no naked numbers.
3. **Segment** — markets aren't monolithic.
4. **Behavioral demand signals** beat stated intent.
5. **Trends with drivers,** not just direction.
6. **State confidence and gaps** — honest uncertainty over false precision.

## Decision framework
- **Investment/sizing decision?** Rigorous TAM/SAM/SOM, top-down and bottom-up reconciled.
- **Product/positioning?** Emphasize segments and unmet needs.
- **New/nascent market?** Bottom-up + analogues; flag high uncertainty.
- **Demand unknown?** Prioritize primary signals (interviews, pre-sales, pilots).

## Common mistakes
- **Naked market-size numbers** with no method or assumptions.
- **Defining the market too broadly** ("it's a $1T market").
- **Only top-down** — no bottom-up reality check.
- **Treating stated interest as demand.**
- **Ignoring segmentation** — averaging away real differences.
- **False precision** hiding genuine uncertainty.

## Validation checklist
- [ ] Market boundary and decision defined.
- [ ] Sizing shown with method (top-down + bottom-up) and assumptions.
- [ ] Customer segments identified with needs.
- [ ] Demand assessed via behavioral signals where possible.
- [ ] Trends and drivers analyzed.
- [ ] Implications drawn; confidence and gaps stated; sources cited.

## Edge cases
- **New category:** no existing market data — build bottom-up from analogues; high uncertainty.
- **B2B niche:** small-N; primary interviews outweigh secondary reports.
- **Rapidly changing markets:** short shelf life; timestamp and note volatility.
- **Conflicting reports:** reconcile methods; report a range, not a single figure.

## Examples
**Input:** "Is there a market for an onboarding tool for mid-market HR teams?"
**Output:** Market defined (NA mid-market, 100–1000 employees, HR onboarding);
sizing — top-down from HR-tech reports and bottom-up (≈X firms × $Y/yr) reconciled to
a SAM range with assumptions shown; segments (high-growth vs. stable); demand signals
(current spend on manual tools, adjacent-tool adoption); trends (remote onboarding);
implications with a stated moderate confidence and the data gaps.

## Automation opportunities
- Pull market data and search/adoption trends to evidence demand.
- Maintain the sizing model with visible assumptions for easy updates.
