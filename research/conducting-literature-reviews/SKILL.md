# Conducting Literature Reviews

## Scope
Systematically surveying and synthesizing existing work on a topic: search →
screen → extract → synthesize into themes, consensus, conflicts, and gaps. Uses
[reading-scientific-papers](../reading-scientific-papers/SKILL.md) per paper and
[synthesizing-research](../synthesizing-research/SKILL.md) for integration.

## Purpose
Turn a scattered body of work into an organized understanding of what's known, where
sources agree and disagree, and what's missing — a synthesis, not a stack of
one-paper summaries strung together.

## When to use this skill
- "Do a literature review / review the research on X / what does the literature say?"
- Surveying existing work before a project, paper, or decision.
- Establishing the state of knowledge on a topic.

## When NOT to use this skill
- Reading one paper → [reading-scientific-papers](../reading-scientific-papers/SKILL.md).
- Appraising one study's quality → [reviewing-research](../../review/reviewing-research/SKILL.md).
- A market (not academic) landscape → [conducting-market-research](../conducting-market-research/SKILL.md).

## Inputs
- The review question/scope and boundaries (topic, timeframe, inclusion criteria).
- Access to the literature and the required rigor (systematic vs. narrative).

## Outputs
- A synthesized review: an extraction matrix of sources, themes with consensus and
  conflicts, identified gaps, and a narrative — with citations.

## Workflow
```
Progress:
- [ ] 1. Define the question, scope, and inclusion/exclusion criteria
- [ ] 2. Search systematically; record terms and sources
- [ ] 3. Screen by criteria; log what's in and out
- [ ] 4. Extract each source into a common matrix
- [ ] 5. Synthesize: themes, consensus, conflicts, gaps
- [ ] 6. Write the review with citations; state limitations
```

**Step 1 — scope and criteria first.** A bounded question and explicit
inclusion/exclusion criteria make the review reproducible and stop it sprawling.
**Step 4 — extract into a matrix** (source × question/method/findings/limitations) so
you can compare across works, not just summarize each. **Step 5 — synthesize by theme,
not by paper.** The deliverable organizes findings around themes and shows where the
literature agrees, disagrees, and is silent — an annotated bibliography (paper-by-
paper) is *not* a literature review. **Step 6 — gaps are a key output:** what hasn't
been studied often matters most.

## Principles
1. **Bounded question + explicit criteria** — reproducible, not sprawling.
2. **Systematic search** — documented terms and sources.
3. **Extract into a matrix** to enable comparison.
4. **Synthesize by theme,** not paper-by-paper.
5. **Surface consensus, conflict, and gaps.**
6. **Cite everything;** state the review's own limitations.

## Decision framework
- **Formal/publishable?** Systematic review with documented protocol and screening counts.
- **Quick orientation?** Narrative review, still theme-organized and cited.
- **Conflicting findings?** Report the disagreement and likely reasons (method, context).
- **Sparse literature?** The gap itself is the finding; say so.

## Common mistakes
- **Annotated list, not synthesis** — summaries with no cross-cutting themes.
- **No scope/criteria** — unbounded and unreproducible.
- **Cherry-picking** supportive studies; ignoring contradictory ones.
- **No matrix** — can't compare across sources.
- **Ignoring gaps** — only reporting what exists.
- **Weak citations** — claims not traceable to sources.

## Validation checklist
- [ ] Question, scope, and inclusion/exclusion criteria defined.
- [ ] Search systematic and documented.
- [ ] Sources screened; extraction matrix built.
- [ ] Synthesis organized by theme with consensus/conflict.
- [ ] Gaps identified.
- [ ] Fully cited; limitations of the review stated.

## Edge cases
- **Huge literature:** narrow scope or focus on reviews/meta-analyses; state the boundary.
- **Interdisciplinary topics:** search multiple field databases; reconcile terminology.
- **Fast-moving fields:** include preprints with caveats; note the as-of date.
- **Conflicting quality:** weight higher-quality studies; note the quality spread.

## Examples
**Input:** "Review the literature on remote-work productivity."
**Output:** Scoped question + criteria (empirical studies, 2018–2025); documented
search; ~30 sources screened to 18; extraction matrix; synthesis by theme (self-
reported vs. measured productivity, role of autonomy, collaboration costs) showing
consensus on focused-task gains, conflict on collaboration, and a gap in long-term
studies — written with citations and stated limitations.
