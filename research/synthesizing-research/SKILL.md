# Synthesizing Research

## Scope
Integrating evidence from many sources into a coherent answer: combining findings,
reconciling conflicts, weighting by quality, and concluding with calibrated
confidence. The synthesis end of research — the counterpart to
[collecting-evidence](../collecting-evidence/SKILL.md) at the start.

## Purpose
Produce a genuine answer, not a pile of summaries: integrate what multiple sources
collectively show, resolve or explain their disagreements, weight by evidence
quality, and state conclusions with honest confidence and full citation.

## When to use this skill
- "Synthesize this research / pull these sources together / what's the overall takeaway?"
- Turning a gathered evidence base into conclusions.
- The final step of a literature review or multi-source investigation.

## When NOT to use this skill
- Gathering the sources → [collecting-evidence](../collecting-evidence/SKILL.md).
- Surveying a field's literature → [conducting-literature-reviews](../conducting-literature-reviews/SKILL.md).
- Summarizing a single document → [summarizing-documents](../../office/summarizing-documents/SKILL.md).

## Inputs
- The evidence base (sources with findings), ideally with credibility assessed.
- The question the synthesis must answer and the audience.

## Outputs
- An integrated, cited synthesis: the overall conclusion, the themes and evidence
  behind it, reconciled conflicts, confidence levels, and remaining uncertainties.

## Workflow
```
Progress:
- [ ] 1. Restate the question the synthesis answers
- [ ] 2. Organize findings by theme/claim, not by source
- [ ] 3. Weight evidence by quality and independence
- [ ] 4. Reconcile conflicts — explain, don't ignore, disagreement
- [ ] 5. Draw conclusions with calibrated confidence
- [ ] 6. State remaining uncertainty; cite everything
```

**Step 2 — organize by claim, not by source.** The defining move of synthesis:
structure around what the evidence says (themes/claims), with sources as support —
not source-by-source summaries. If your output is "Source A says…, Source B says…",
that's a summary, not a synthesis. **Step 3 — weight by quality:** three independent
high-quality studies outweigh ten low-quality echoes; corroboration across
independent sources strengthens a claim. **Step 4 — reconcile conflicts honestly:**
where sources disagree, explain why (method, context, recency, bias) rather than
picking one or averaging them. **Step 5 — calibrate confidence:** "strong evidence
that", "limited evidence suggests", "sources conflict on" — match certainty to the
weight of evidence, and never overstate.

## Principles
1. **Integrate, don't list** — organize by claim, not by source.
2. **Weight by quality and independence,** not by count.
3. **Reconcile conflicts explicitly;** disagreement is a finding.
4. **Calibrate confidence** to the evidence.
5. **Distinguish evidence from inference** — mark where you're reasoning beyond sources.
6. **Cite every claim;** state what remains uncertain.

## Decision framework
- **Strong convergent evidence?** State the conclusion with confidence.
- **Sources conflict?** Present the disagreement and the likely reason; conclude conditionally or not at all.
- **Thin/low-quality evidence?** Say so; a weak "we don't really know" beats false confidence.
- **Going beyond the evidence?** Label it as inference/opinion, not finding.

## Common mistakes
- **Source-by-source summary** masquerading as synthesis.
- **Counting sources** instead of weighting quality/independence.
- **Ignoring or hiding conflicting evidence.**
- **Overstated confidence** beyond what the evidence supports.
- **Blurring evidence and your own inference.**
- **Uncited conclusions** — claims not traceable.

## Validation checklist
- [ ] The question is restated and answered.
- [ ] Organized by theme/claim, not by source.
- [ ] Evidence weighted by quality and independence.
- [ ] Conflicts reconciled or explained.
- [ ] Conclusions carry calibrated confidence.
- [ ] Evidence vs. inference distinguished; uncertainty stated.
- [ ] Every claim cited.

## Edge cases
- **Contradictory high-quality sources:** present the genuine disagreement; don't force a conclusion.
- **Mostly low-quality evidence:** conclude tentatively and flag the weakness.
- **Rapidly evolving topic:** note the as-of date; conclusions may shift.
- **Sparse evidence:** "insufficient evidence to conclude" is a legitimate output.

## Examples
**Input:** "Synthesize what these 12 sources say about four-day work weeks."
**Output:** Organized by claim: **strong evidence** that focused-task productivity
holds or improves (5 independent studies incl. 2 trials); **mixed evidence** on
collaboration-heavy work (sources conflict — likely due to role differences);
**limited evidence** on long-term effects (few longitudinal studies). Overall: net
positive for suitable roles, with calibrated confidence, conflicts explained, gaps
noted, and every claim cited — not a source-by-source recap.
