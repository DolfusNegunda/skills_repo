# Analyzing Competitors

## Scope
Understanding the competitive landscape and what it means for your strategy:
identifying competitors (direct, indirect, potential), profiling each, and drawing
implications. Feeds strategy, positioning, and business cases. General like-for-like
comparison is [conducting-comparative-research](../conducting-comparative-research/SKILL.md).

## Purpose
Turn competitor information into strategic insight: not just "here's what they do" but
"here's where we can win, what threatens us, and what we should do about it."

## When to use this skill
- "Do a competitive analysis / analyze our competitors / how do we compare?"
- Mapping a market's players and positioning.
- Informing strategy, positioning, pricing, or a business case.

## When NOT to use this skill
- Generic option comparison → [conducting-comparative-research](../conducting-comparative-research/SKILL.md).
- Market size/demand → [conducting-market-research](../conducting-market-research/SKILL.md).
- Internal strategic position → [running-swot-analysis](../../business/running-swot-analysis/SKILL.md).

## Inputs
- Your business/product and the market/segment; the strategic question.
- Sources: competitor sites, pricing, reviews, filings, press, analyst reports.

## Outputs
- A competitive analysis: competitor set, per-competitor profiles (positioning,
  strengths/weaknesses, strategy), a comparison, and strategic implications for you.

## Workflow
```
Progress:
- [ ] 1. Define the market/segment and the strategic question
- [ ] 2. Identify competitors: direct, indirect, and potential
- [ ] 3. Profile each: positioning, offering, pricing, strengths/weaknesses
- [ ] 4. Compare on the dimensions that matter to customers
- [ ] 5. Assess each competitor's likely strategy and trajectory
- [ ] 6. Draw implications: opportunities, threats, and actions
```

**Step 2 — look beyond direct rivals.** Indirect competitors (different solution, same
need) and potential entrants often matter more than obvious rivals. **Step 3 —
evidence, not impression:** profile from real signals (pricing pages, reviews,
job postings, filings), and note where the competitor is genuinely strong, not just
what you'd like to believe. **Step 6 — implications are the deliverable.** A landscape
with no "so what" is wasted; convert findings into where you can differentiate, where
you're exposed, and what to do.

## Principles
1. **Insight, not inventory** — end with implications and actions.
2. **Direct + indirect + potential** competitors.
3. **Evidence-based profiles** from real signals.
4. **Compare on what customers value,** not internal feature lists.
5. **Honest about competitor strengths** — wishful analysis is dangerous.
6. **Timestamp it** — competitive positions shift.

## Decision framework
- **Positioning decision?** Focus on differentiation and gaps in the map.
- **Pricing decision?** Deep-dive pricing/packaging across the set.
- **Threat assessment?** Weight trajectory and potential entrants.
- **Many competitors?** Cluster into strategic groups; profile the key ones.

## Common mistakes
- **Feature table with no conclusion** — data, no insight.
- **Only direct competitors** — blindsided by indirect/new entrants.
- **Underrating competitors** — comforting but dangerous.
- **Impressions over evidence.**
- **Comparing internal features** customers don't care about.
- **One-time snapshot** treated as permanent.

## Validation checklist
- [ ] Market/segment and strategic question defined.
- [ ] Direct, indirect, and potential competitors identified.
- [ ] Each profiled from evidence (positioning, offer, pricing, strengths/weaknesses).
- [ ] Compared on customer-relevant dimensions.
- [ ] Likely strategy/trajectory assessed.
- [ ] Strategic implications and actions drawn; recency noted.

## Edge cases
- **Private competitors:** limited data — triangulate from reviews, jobs, and press; flag uncertainty.
- **Fast-moving markets:** short shelf life; schedule refreshes.
- **New/creating a category:** focus on substitutes and the status-quo alternative.
- **Own bias:** guard against dismissing a competitor you compete with emotionally.

## Examples
**Input:** "Competitive analysis for our project-management SaaS."
**Output:** Competitor set (direct: two incumbents; indirect: spreadsheets +
general tools; potential: a well-funded entrant); evidence-based profiles
(positioning, pricing tiers, review-sourced strengths/weaknesses); comparison on
customer-valued dimensions (ease, integrations, price); trajectory notes; and
implications — a mid-market ease-of-use gap we can own, a pricing threat from the
entrant, and three recommended actions.
