# Studying Effectively

## Scope
Working through material so it *sticks*: active recall, spaced repetition, the Feynman
technique, interleaving, and retrieval practice, chosen over passive rereading and
highlighting. Covers how to study, not what to study or in what order — that is the
curriculum ([planning-learning](../planning-learning/SKILL.md)).

## Purpose
Convert study time into durable understanding and recall by forcing retrieval and
spacing it over time, instead of re-exposing yourself to material and mistaking
familiarity for knowledge.

## When to use this skill
- "Help me study / remember / retain X"; "I keep forgetting what I read."
- Preparing for an exam, interview, or certification.
- Any material that must be recalled or applied later, not just skimmed once.

## When NOT to use this skill
- Planning the overall curriculum or roadmap → [planning-learning](../planning-learning/SKILL.md).
- Reading research papers specifically → [reading-scientific-papers](../../research/reading-scientific-papers/SKILL.md).
- Capturing knowledge for reference (not recall) → [managing-personal-knowledge](../managing-personal-knowledge/SKILL.md).
- Note-taking mechanics → [taking-notes](../taking-notes/SKILL.md).

## Inputs
- The material and the target: recall, application, or deep understanding.
- The deadline (exam date, interview) and time available to study.
- Current grasp of the material and where it feels shaky.

## Outputs
- A study routine built on retrieval and spacing, not rereading.
- Recall artifacts: question sets, flashcards, or blank-page explanations.
- A spaced review schedule and a way to track what is not yet solid.

## Workflow
```
Progress:
- [ ] 1. Set the retention target and deadline
- [ ] 2. First pass for understanding, then close the material
- [ ] 3. Retrieve from memory — recall before rereading
- [ ] 4. Explain it simply (Feynman) to expose gaps
- [ ] 5. Space reviews over increasing intervals
- [ ] 6. Interleave related topics instead of blocking one
- [ ] 7. Track weak spots and re-test them more often
```

**Step 1 — Set the target.** Decide whether the goal is recall, application, or
understanding, and by when — this sets how hard and how often to test.

**Step 2 — Understand, then close.** Read once for comprehension, then *shut the book*.
Study happens when the material is out of sight, not while re-scanning it.

**Step 3 — Retrieve.** Recall from a blank page or answer questions from memory before
looking back. Retrieval is the mechanism that builds memory; rereading only builds
false confidence. Check against the source only after attempting.

**Step 4 — Feynman.** Explain the concept in plain language as if teaching a beginner.
Where the explanation stalls or turns vague is exactly the gap to restudy.

**Step 5 — Space it.** Review at expanding intervals (e.g. 1 day, 3 days, a week)
rather than in one block. Spacing beats cramming for anything you must keep past the
exam; cramming fades within days.

**Step 6 — Interleave.** Mix related topics/problem types in a session instead of
drilling one to exhaustion. Interleaving trains you to *choose* the right approach,
which blocked practice never tests.

**Step 7 — Track weak spots.** Log what you missed and re-test those items more often.
Effortful items need more retrievals; easy ones can space out further.

## Principles
1. **Retrieval over review** — pulling from memory beats re-reading it in.
2. **Difficulty is the point** — effortful recall is what builds durable memory.
3. **Space, don't cram** — distributed practice outlasts a single long session.
4. **Explain to expose gaps** — if it can't be said simply, it isn't understood.
5. **Interleave to discriminate** — mixing trains selection, not just execution.
6. **Feedback every loop** — check against the source; correct errors immediately.
7. **Test, don't judge familiarity** — recognizing material is not knowing it.

## Decision framework
- **Rereading and it feels easy?** Stop — close the book and self-test instead.
- **Must retain past the deadline?** Space reviews; don't cram the night before.
- **Confusing similar concepts?** Interleave and contrast them side by side.
- **Not sure you understand?** Feynman it — teach it out loud with no notes.
- **Limited time?** Spend it on retrieval of weak items, not re-reading strong ones.

## Common mistakes
- **Rereading and highlighting** instead of recalling — the most common trap.
- **Passive consumption** — watching/reading with no practice or output produced.
- **Cramming** — one massed session that fades within days instead of spacing.
- **No feedback loop** — never checking recall against the source to catch errors.
- **Blocking one topic** to mastery, then failing to mix it under real conditions.
- **Mistaking familiarity for knowledge** — "I've seen this" is not "I can produce this."
- **Over-making flashcards** as busywork without actually testing against them.

## Validation checklist
- [ ] Retention target and deadline are set.
- [ ] Study is done with the material closed, via retrieval.
- [ ] Recall is checked against the source and errors corrected.
- [ ] Reviews are spaced over expanding intervals, not massed.
- [ ] Related topics are interleaved, not blocked one at a time.
- [ ] Concepts are explained plainly (Feynman) to surface gaps.
- [ ] Weak items are tracked and re-tested more frequently.

## Edge cases
- **Rote facts (vocab, formulas):** lean on spaced-repetition flashcards.
- **Conceptual understanding:** weight Feynman explanation and applied problems.
- **Tight deadline:** still prefer retrieval + interleaving over rereading; accept some cramming only for short-term recall.
- **Skill (not knowledge):** practice the skill itself with feedback ([planning-learning](../planning-learning/SKILL.md)).
- **Dense source material:** capture it once as notes ([taking-notes](../taking-notes/SKILL.md)), then study from questions, not the notes.

## Examples
**Input:** "I have two weeks to study for a certification and I keep forgetting what I read."
**Output:** Diagnosis: passive rereading. Routine: read each topic once, then close
it and write everything recalled on a blank page; convert misses into flashcards.
Feynman each concept aloud. Space reviews at 1/3/7-day intervals across the two weeks,
interleaving topic areas per session rather than one topic per day. Track missed items
and re-test them daily until solid — the last days reinforce weak spots, not a cram.

## Automation opportunities
- Use a spaced-repetition app (SRS) to schedule flashcard reviews automatically.
- Set recurring calendar blocks at expanding intervals for review sessions.
- Keep a running "missed items" list that feeds the next retrieval session.
