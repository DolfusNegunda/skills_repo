# Setting and Tracking Goals

## Scope
Turning a personal aspiration into a goal that is followed through: naming the
outcome, defining the process behavior that drives it, choosing a leading measure,
tracking habits or streaks, and reviewing on a cadence. Personal scope — not
organizational objectives or project delivery.

## Purpose
Convert vague intentions into a goal with a controllable behavior behind it and a
rhythm that keeps it alive, so progress is visible early and course-corrected
before it's too late — beating the usual fade after week one.

## When to use this skill
- Setting a personal goal (fitness, learning, savings, a creative habit).
- A past goal fizzled and needs a structure that sticks.
- Wanting progress visible now, not only at a distant deadline.

## When NOT to use this skill
- Organizational objectives and key results → [setting-okrs](../../business/setting-okrs/SKILL.md).
- Project milestones and delivery plans → [planning-projects](../../business/planning-projects/SKILL.md).
- Day-level task scheduling → [planning-your-day](../planning-your-day/SKILL.md).

## Inputs
- The aspiration and why it matters now.
- A candidate outcome, and the behavior most likely to produce it.
- Current baseline, a target, and a realistic time horizon.

## Outputs
- One outcome goal plus the process goal (behavior) that drives it.
- A leading measure and a simple habit/streak tracker.
- A set review cadence with dates, and adjustments recorded at each review.

## Workflow
```
Progress:
- [ ] 1. Name the aspiration and why it matters now
- [ ] 2. Write one outcome goal with a target and horizon
- [ ] 3. Define the process goal — the behavior that drives the outcome
- [ ] 4. Pick a leading measure and set up a habit/streak tracker
- [ ] 5. Set a review cadence with concrete dates
- [ ] 6. Review on cadence: read the trend, adjust, keep or retire the goal
```

**Step 1 — Motivate.** State the aspiration and why now. A goal without a live
reason gets dropped first; keep it to the few that genuinely matter.

**Step 2 — Outcome goal.** Write one measurable outcome with a target and horizon
("run a 10k in under 55 min by November"). Limit active goals — a handful at most,
so attention isn't spread thin.

**Step 3 — Process goal.** Define the behavior you control that produces the
outcome ("run 3x/week"). Outcomes lag and depend on luck; the process is what you
can act on daily. Break big goals down with
[breaking-down-tasks](../breaking-down-tasks/SKILL.md).

**Step 4 — Measure and track.** Pick a *leading* measure (sessions done this week),
not only the *lagging* result (race time). Track it simply — a streak, a checkbox
grid, a running count. Anchor the behavior to existing routines
([managing-time](../managing-time/SKILL.md), [managing-tasks](../managing-tasks/SKILL.md)).

**Step 5 — Cadence.** Put review dates on the calendar (weekly for process, monthly
for outcome). Without a cadence the goal drifts out of sight.

**Step 6 — Review.** Read the trend, not a single data point. Adjust the target or
the behavior, and be willing to retire a goal that no longer matters rather than
carry it as guilt.

## Principles
1. **Pair every outcome with a process goal** — you act on behavior, not results.
2. **Track a leading measure,** so problems show up early enough to fix.
3. **Few goals at once** — focus beats a long, ignored list.
4. **Make progress visible** — a streak or grid you see daily.
5. **Review on a fixed cadence,** or the goal quietly dies.
6. **Retire stale goals** deliberately; a goal is a tool, not a debt.

## Decision framework
- **Outcome or process?** Set both — the outcome names the destination, the process gets you there.
- **Leading or lagging measure?** Prefer leading; keep the lagging one only as the scoreboard.
- **More than a few active goals?** Cut to the ones that matter now.
- **Falling behind at review?** Shrink the behavior before abandoning the goal.
- **Goal no longer meaningful?** Retire it on purpose instead of letting it rot.

## Common mistakes
- **Only lagging measures** — the number moves too late to act on.
- **Outcome goal with no process behind it** — a wish, not a plan.
- **Too many goals at once,** so none get real attention.
- **No review cadence,** so the goal fades after the first week.
- **Tracking nothing,** so progress is invisible and motivation stalls.
- **Clinging to a stale goal** out of guilt instead of retiring it.

## Validation checklist
- [ ] Each outcome goal is paired with a controllable process goal.
- [ ] A leading measure is chosen, not only a lagging result.
- [ ] Active goals are few enough to sustain attention.
- [ ] A habit/streak tracker makes progress visible.
- [ ] A review cadence with dates is set and honored.
- [ ] Reviews read the trend and adjust or retire the goal.

## Edge cases
- **Purely outcome-driven goal (e.g. weight):** still define the daily behavior that moves it.
- **Long-horizon goal:** add interim milestones so feedback arrives before the deadline.
- **Streak broken:** resume next day; one miss isn't failure — an abandoned tracker is.
- **Learning goal:** pair with [planning-learning](../planning-learning/SKILL.md) and [studying-effectively](../studying-effectively/SKILL.md).

## Examples
**Input:** "I want to get fit this year but I always give up by February."
**Output:** Outcome: "run a 10k under 55 min by November." Process: "run 3x/week."
Leading measure: runs completed this week (target 3); lagging: current 10k time.
Tracker: a weekly streak grid. Cadence: 5-min weekly review of runs done, monthly
check of pace trend. At review the runs, not the race, drive adjustments — if three
is too many, drop to two rather than quit.

## Automation opportunities
- A habit-tracking app or spreadsheet that computes streaks and weekly totals.
- Recurring calendar events for the weekly and monthly reviews.
- Reminders tied to the process behavior (e.g. run days) rather than the outcome.
