# Keeping Decision Journals

## Scope
Capturing consequential decisions at the moment they are made — the situation,
the reasoning, the alternatives, and the predicted result — and reviewing entries
once outcomes are known, so judgment improves over time. Covers the personal
practice, not the choosing itself or organizational record-keeping.

## Purpose
Preserve what was actually believed and expected *before* the outcome, so later
review measures the quality of the reasoning rather than a memory rewritten by
hindsight. The journal turns scattered guesses into calibrated judgment.

## When to use this skill
- Facing a decision whose outcome will be visible weeks or months later.
- Wanting to learn from past choices instead of misremembering them.
- Noticing a pattern of "I knew it all along" after the fact.

## When NOT to use this skill
- Making the decision itself → [deciding-under-uncertainty](../../reasoning/deciding-under-uncertainty/SKILL.md).
- Formal governance or audit records → [establishing-governance](../../business/establishing-governance/SKILL.md).
- Trivial or fully reversible choices — the logging cost outweighs the learning.

## Inputs
- The decision to be made and the situation forcing it.
- The realistic options and what is known and unknown right now.
- A concrete, checkable prediction and the date it can be judged.

## Outputs
- A dated entry: context, options, chosen option, rationale, expected outcome, confidence.
- A review note comparing prediction to reality, with the lesson extracted.
- A growing record that reveals recurring biases and blind spots.

## Workflow
```
Progress:
- [ ] 1. Capture the decision and its context at the moment of deciding
- [ ] 2. List the real options and the rationale for the choice
- [ ] 3. State a checkable expected outcome, confidence, and review date
- [ ] 4. Store the entry where it will actually be found again
- [ ] 5. Review on the due date: prediction vs reality
- [ ] 6. Extract the lesson and look for patterns across entries
```

**Step 1 — Capture.** Write the entry *before* the outcome is known. Record the
date, the decision, and the situation forcing it — what pressure, constraints, and
information were present. Memory of this erodes fast; capture it live.

**Step 2 — Options and rationale.** List the options genuinely considered, not just
the one taken, and *why* this one won. The rejected paths are what make review
informative later.

**Step 3 — Predict.** State a concrete, checkable expected outcome ("ship by Q3",
"churn under 5%"), a confidence level, and the date it can be judged. A vague
prediction cannot be scored.

**Step 4 — Store.** Keep entries in one durable, searchable place tied to your
notes ([managing-personal-knowledge](../managing-personal-knowledge/SKILL.md),
[taking-notes](../taking-notes/SKILL.md)). An unfindable journal is never reviewed.

**Step 5 — Review.** On the due date, compare the prediction to what happened
*before* rereading your rationale, so the outcome does not recolor the reasoning.

**Step 6 — Learn.** Note whether the reasoning was sound regardless of luck, and
scan multiple entries for recurring biases (overconfidence, anchoring, sunk cost).

## Principles
1. **Write before the outcome** — the entry is worthless if reconstructed later.
2. **Rationale and prediction are the point,** not just the choice logged.
3. **Judge the reasoning, not only the result** — good calls can have bad luck.
4. **Predictions must be checkable** — with a value and a date.
5. **Review is non-negotiable** — an unreviewed journal is a diary, not a tool.
6. **One durable home** so entries are actually found again.

## Decision framework
- **Is the choice consequential and non-trivial?** If not, skip the entry.
- **Can the expected outcome be checked later?** If not, sharpen it until it can.
- **Outcome known but reasoning was flawed?** Record it as a process lesson, not a win.
- **Outcome bad but reasoning was sound?** Log it as good judgment, bad luck — don't over-correct.
- **No review date set?** The entry is incomplete; add one.

## Common mistakes
- **Logging the choice but not the rationale or expected outcome** — nothing to calibrate against.
- **Writing the entry after the outcome,** so hindsight rewrites it.
- **Never reviewing** the journal — the whole payoff is in the review.
- **Vague predictions** ("it'll go fine") that can't be scored.
- **Judging only by results,** rewarding lucky bad decisions and punishing unlucky good ones.
- **Scattering entries** across apps until none are findable.

## Validation checklist
- [ ] Entry written at decision time, before the outcome is known.
- [ ] Context, options, chosen option, and rationale all recorded.
- [ ] Expected outcome is concrete and checkable, with a confidence level.
- [ ] A review date is set on every entry.
- [ ] Reviews compare prediction to reality and separate reasoning from luck.
- [ ] Entries live in one durable, searchable place.

## Edge cases
- **Reversible or low-stakes decision:** skip it; reserve the journal for calls that matter.
- **Long horizon before outcome:** set an interim checkpoint so the entry isn't forgotten.
- **Group decision:** log your own reasoning and prediction separately from the consensus.
- **Sensitive content:** keep the journal private; redact names if it may be shared.

## Examples
**Input:** "I'm about to accept a job offer — how do I log this decision?"
**Output:** Entry dated today: context (current role stalling, two offers), options
(stay, offer A, offer B), chosen (offer A), rationale (growth + comp), expected
outcome ("more autonomy and a promotion path within 12 months", confidence 70%),
review date set to +12 months. At review: compare reality to the prediction before
rereading the rationale; note whether the reasoning held regardless of how it turned out.

## Automation opportunities
- A template or snippet with the fixed fields so entries are fast and consistent.
- A calendar or task reminder auto-created from each entry's review date.
- A periodic prompt to scan recent reviews for recurring bias patterns.
