# Taking Notes

## Scope
The craft of taking notes that pay off later: choosing a method for the situation,
capturing meaning in your own words, and processing raw notes into a system. Covers
the note itself; the surrounding system is a separate skill.

## Purpose
Produce notes you'll actually use — understandable and actionable weeks later —
instead of a wall of transcription you never reopen. Notes are for future you.

## When to use this skill
- Taking notes from reading, a lecture, a talk, a video, or self-study.
- Notes get captured but are never revisited or turned into anything.
- Choosing a note method for a given source or purpose.
- Processing rough notes into a keep-worthy form.

## When NOT to use this skill
- Formal meeting minutes (decisions, actions, attendees) → [recording-meeting-minutes](../../office/recording-meeting-minutes/SKILL.md).
- Condensing a transcript someone supplied → [summarizing-meeting-notes](../../office/summarizing-meeting-notes/SKILL.md).
- Storing, linking, and retrieving notes at scale → [managing-personal-knowledge](../managing-personal-knowledge/SKILL.md).

## Inputs
- The source (reading, talk, lecture) and why you're taking notes.
- How the notes will be used later (study, reference, writing, action).
- Your capture tool and how much attention the live moment allows.

## Outputs
- Notes in a fit-for-purpose method, in your own words.
- Captured questions, actions, and connections — not just facts.
- Processed notes filed into your system with a clear title.

## Workflow
```
Progress:
- [ ] 1. Set the purpose: how these notes will be used later
- [ ] 2. Pick a method to match (Cornell, outline, atomic)
- [ ] 3. Capture meaning in your own words, not a transcript
- [ ] 4. Mark questions, actions, and connections as you go
- [ ] 5. Process soon: clarify, title, and file into your system
- [ ] 6. Review to confirm it makes sense without the source
```

**Step 1 — Purpose.** Decide upfront how you'll use the notes. Study, reference, and
writing demand different notes; purpose drives every later choice.

**Step 2 — Method.** Match the method: **Cornell** (cue/notes/summary) for
lectures and study; **outline** for structured talks; **atomic/Zettelkasten**
(one idea per note, in your words) for building durable, linkable knowledge.

**Step 3 — Capture meaning.** Write in your own words — paraphrasing forces
understanding. Record the point, not every word; verbatim is a transcript, not a note.

**Step 4 — Mark.** Flag open questions, action items, and links to what you already
know. These hooks are what make a note worth reopening.

**Step 5 — Process.** Soon after (same day), clean up shorthand, give the note a
descriptive title, and file it into your system. Raw notes decay fast.

**Step 6 — Review.** Reread as future-you: does it stand alone without the source?
If not, add the missing context now while you still remember it.

## Principles
1. **Notes are for future you** — the test is later usefulness, not live coverage.
2. **Meaning over transcript** — paraphrase; understanding beats stenography.
3. **Match method to purpose** — one size does not fit lecture, talk, and study.
4. **Capture hooks** — questions, actions, and connections, not just facts.
5. **Process while warm** — unprocessed notes rot within days.
6. **One idea per atomic note** — small, self-contained notes link and reuse well.

## Decision framework
- **Which method?** Lecture/study → Cornell; structured talk → outline; building knowledge → atomic.
- **Write it verbatim?** Only exact quotes/definitions; otherwise paraphrase.
- **This a note or a task?** If it's an action, route it to your task system.
- **Live capture too fast?** Capture keywords now, expand within the hour.
- **Formal record needed?** Switch to [recording-meeting-minutes](../../office/recording-meeting-minutes/SKILL.md).

## Common mistakes
- **Transcribing verbatim** — a wall of text you never reread and didn't absorb.
- **Notes never revisited or processed** — captured and abandoned in the raw.
- **No purpose** — method and detail chosen at random, useless for the real need.
- **Highlighting as note-taking** — passive marking without your own words.
- **Missing the hooks** — facts logged, but no questions, actions, or links.
- **Untitled dumps** — notes that can't be found or understood later.

## Validation checklist
- [ ] Purpose was set before capturing.
- [ ] Method fits the source and purpose.
- [ ] Notes are in your own words, not verbatim.
- [ ] Questions, actions, and connections are marked.
- [ ] Notes were processed and titled soon after capture.
- [ ] The note stands alone and is filed where it'll be found.

## Edge cases
- **Fast live source:** capture keywords/cues live, expand within the hour.
- **Dense technical material:** capture worked examples and your own restatement, not just claims.
- **Audio/video recorded:** note timestamps for later, don't try to transcribe live.
- **Action-heavy source:** split — actions to the task system, ideas to notes.

## Examples
**Input:** "I take pages of notes in lectures but they're useless at exam time."
**Output:** Switched to Cornell: notes on the right, cue questions in the left
margin, a two-line summary at the bottom. Stopped transcribing — paraphrased each
point instead. Marked three open questions to resolve after class. Same evening,
titled and filed the page, tested recall by covering the notes and answering the
cues. The page now works as an exam study sheet on its own.

## Automation opportunities
- Use a Cornell/outline template so structure is automatic.
- Auto-sync highlights and margin notes from your reader into the inbox.
- Use voice capture for keywords when writing isn't possible, then expand.
