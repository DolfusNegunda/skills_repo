## Tone registers

| Register | Sounds like | Contractions | Use for |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Formal | "I am writing to confirm..." | No | Executives, legal/HR matters, first contact with senior external people, anything that may be forwarded widely |
| Professional-neutral | "Confirming the timeline we discussed..." | Sparingly | Default for external partners and clients you already work with |
| Warm | "Great to hear from you — happy to help with this." | Yes | Colleagues, long-standing clients, good-news messages |
| Concise/internal | "Two things: budget is approved; kickoff moves to Tue." | Yes | Internal peers, fast-moving threads, status pings |
| Warm-but-firm | "I understand the pressure here, and the answer is still no for this quarter." | Yes | Declines, boundary-setting, second follow-ups, pushing back on scope |

Register is set by three dials: **word choice** (regret → sorry), **sentence
length** (formal runs longer), and **contractions**. Adjust all three together;
mixing them ("I am writing to say thx") reads as careless.

## Choosing a register

- Unknown recipient or high stakes → start one notch more formal than feels
  natural; you can warm up in later messages but can't easily formalise.
- Replying → mirror the sender's register, then shift at most one notch toward
  where you need the conversation to go.
- Bad news travels best in warm-but-firm: acknowledge, decide, offer a path.
- Anger never goes in an email. If the honest draft is angry, write the
  professional-neutral version and put feelings in a call.

## Length budgets

| Message type | Budget |
| --- | --- |
| Status ping, scheduling, quick confirm | 30–60 words |
| Standard request, follow-up, decline | 60–120 words |
| Apology, escalation, cold intro | 100–180 words |
| Anything longer | Question whether it should be a doc + short cover email |

If the body needs more than three short paragraphs, move detail to an
attachment or bullet list and keep the prose to decision + ask.

## Subject-line rules

1. **Topic + action, under ~8 words.** "Q3 forecast — review by Thu" beats
   "Forecast" beats "Quick question".
2. **Front-load the noun** the recipient will search for later (project name,
   invoice number, client).
3. **Put the deadline in the subject** when there is one: "Contract redlines —
   sign-off needed by 15 Jul".
4. **Signal the required effort** where helpful: "FYI — no action needed" or
   "Decision needed: vendor A vs B".
5. **Replies keep the thread's subject.** Change it only when the topic truly
   changes, and mark it: "Re: Kickoff (was: Intro call)".
6. Never: ALL CAPS, "URGENT!!!", bare "Hello", or a subject that repeats the
   whole first sentence.

## Scenario library

Each pattern gives the skeleton; fill it from the user's brief. Tone noted per
scenario.

### Declining a request
Tone: warm-but-firm. Budget: 60–120 words.
1. Thank them / acknowledge the request specifically (not a generic "thanks
   for reaching out").
2. Decline clearly in one sentence. Use "can't" or "won't be able to" — not
   "it might be difficult", which invites negotiation.
3. Give one honest reason. One. Multiple reasons each become a target for rebuttal.
4. Offer an alternative if one genuinely exists (later date, another contact,
   a smaller scope). Skip this step rather than invent a fake olive branch.
5. Close forward-looking, not apologetic.

### Chasing / following up
Tone: professional-neutral; firmer with each round. Budget: 30–90 words.
- **First nudge (after ~3–5 business days):** assume good faith. Restate the
  ask and deadline in one line; re-attach or link whatever they need so they
  can act without digging. "Bumping this in case it slipped past."
- **Second follow-up:** add consequence, not guilt: what is blocked and by
  when you need an answer. Offer an easy out: "If someone else is the right
  person, happy to redirect."
- **Final notice:** state what happens by default: "If I don't hear back by
  Friday, I'll proceed with option A / close this out." Then actually do it.
- Never open with "As per my last email" or "Just checking in" — the first is
  passive-aggressive, the second buries the ask.

### Apologising for a delay
Tone: professional-neutral or warm. Budget: 100–180 words.
1. Apologise once, plainly, in the first sentence: "I'm sorry the report is
   late." No passive voice ("apologies for any inconvenience caused").
2. Brief, honest reason — one sentence, no saga, no blaming a third party.
3. The fix: new concrete date/time, and what you're doing to hit it.
4. Prevention, if it's a repeat risk: what changes so it doesn't recur.
5. Do not re-apologise in the closing. End on the commitment.

### Escalation
Tone: formal or professional-neutral — facts, not heat. Budget: 100–180 words.
1. Subject names the issue and the need: "Payment gateway outage — decision
   needed today".
2. One-sentence summary of the problem and its business impact (money, dates,
   customers — quantify if possible).
3. What has already been tried, with dates. This is what makes it an
   escalation and not a complaint.
4. The specific decision or action requested from *this* recipient, with a
   deadline.
5. Neutral phrasing about people: "the response is pending since 24 Jun", not
   "Sam has been ignoring me". CC the people involved rather than reporting on
   them behind their backs.

### Cold introduction
Tone: professional-neutral, slightly warm. Budget: 60–120 words — shorter is
disproportionately effective here.
1. Subject makes relevance obvious: mutual contact, event, or their work —
   "Intro via Maria Chen — supplier onboarding".
2. First line: why *them*, specifically. A line proving you know their work
   beats a paragraph about yourself.
3. One or two sentences on who you are and the single reason you're writing.
4. A small, specific ask: 20 minutes, one answer, one referral — never "pick
   your brain" or "explore synergies".
5. Make refusal easy: "If this isn't relevant, no reply needed."

### Scheduling
Tone: concise/internal or professional-neutral. Budget: 30–60 words.
- Proposing: offer 2–3 concrete slots **with timezone**, state duration and
  purpose, and name a fallback ("or send times that suit you").
- Rescheduling: apologise once, take the blame ("I need to move our call"),
  and immediately propose new slots — never leave it at "can we reschedule?".
- Confirming: repeat date, time, timezone, duration, and channel/location in
  one line so the email doubles as the record.

## Sign-off ladder

| Register | Sign-off |
| --- | --- |
| Formal | Kind regards / Sincerely |
| Professional-neutral | Best regards / Best |
| Warm / internal | Thanks / Cheers / Best |

Match the sign-off to the body's register. "Warm regards" after a firm decline
undercuts the message; "Sincerely" on a two-line internal ping is stiff.
