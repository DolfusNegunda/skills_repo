# Editing Prose

## Substantive editing, not proofreading

## Scope
Improving *how* existing writing works: clarity, concision, sentence and paragraph
flow, structure, and tone. Sits between drafting and the final
[proofreading-text](../proofreading-text/SKILL.md) pass. Not surface correction and
not writing from scratch.

## Purpose
Make the writing clearer, tighter, and better organized while keeping the author's
core message and (usually) voice — and explain the changes so the author learns.

## When to use this skill
- "Edit / improve / tighten / polish this."
- "Make it clearer / flow better / shorter / more formal / less formal."
- Prose that is correct but wordy, buried, disorganized, or off-tone.

## When NOT to use this skill
- Only typos/grammar → [proofreading-text](../proofreading-text/SKILL.md).
- Nothing drafted yet → the relevant writing skill.
- Wholesale rewrite/new argument → drafting, not editing.

## Inputs
- The draft, its purpose and audience, and the target tone/length.
- How heavy an edit is wanted (light touch vs. deep restructure) and whether to
  preserve voice strictly.

## Outputs
- The edited text, plus a brief rationale for the significant changes (what and
  why), preserving the author's intent.

## Workflow
```
Progress:
- [ ] 1. Read for intent: what is the author trying to say, to whom?
- [ ] 2. Structure: is the order logical? Point up front?
- [ ] 3. Paragraphs: one idea each; smooth transitions
- [ ] 4. Sentences: tighten, activate, vary length, cut filler
- [ ] 5. Word choice: precise, plain, consistent tone
- [ ] 6. Preserve voice and message; explain major changes
- [ ] 7. Hand off to proofreading for the final pass
```

**Step 1 — Intent first.** Understand the message and audience before changing
anything; editing against the wrong goal makes it worse.

**Step 2 — Structure (biggest lever).** Fix order before wording. Is the main point
up front? Do sections follow logically? Reorganizing weak structure improves a piece
more than any sentence-level polish.

**Step 3 — Paragraphs.** One idea per paragraph; strong topic sentences; transitions
that carry the reader from each point to the next.

**Step 4 — Sentences.** Cut filler and redundancy, convert passive to active where
it helps, break up overlong sentences, and vary rhythm. Aim for fewer words carrying
more meaning.

**Step 5 — Words.** Replace vague/abstract words with precise concrete ones; remove
jargon the audience won't share; keep tone consistent.

**Step 6 — Voice & rationale.** Preserve the author's voice and core message unless
asked to change them. For significant edits, say what you changed and why so the
author can accept/reject and learn.

**Step 7 — Proofread.** The final surface pass is [proofreading-text](../proofreading-text/SKILL.md).

## Principles
1. **Structure before sentences before words.** Biggest gains come from the top level.
2. **Preserve the message and voice;** improve the expression.
3. **Every word earns its place;** concision is clarity.
4. **Edit for the reader,** not to sound clever.
5. **Explain significant changes** so the author retains control and learns.

## Decision framework
- **Disorganized?** Restructure first — don't polish sentences in the wrong order.
- **Wordy?** Cut ruthlessly; target a word-count reduction.
- **Wrong tone?** Adjust register (formality, warmth) consistently throughout.
- **Author's voice matters (op-ed, personal)?** Light touch, preserve idiosyncrasy.
- **Correct but final?** It's a proofread, not an edit.

## Common mistakes
- **Polishing sentences in a broken structure** — reorganize first.
- **Overwriting the author's voice** into a generic style.
- **Changing the meaning** while "improving" wording.
- **Editing without knowing the audience/purpose.**
- **Silent wholesale rewrites** with no rationale — the author loses trust and control.
- **Adding words** ("in order to", "the fact that") instead of cutting them.

## Validation checklist
- [ ] Main point is early and the structure is logical.
- [ ] Each paragraph has one idea and smooth transitions.
- [ ] Sentences are tighter and mostly active; filler removed.
- [ ] Word choice is precise; tone is consistent and audience-appropriate.
- [ ] Core message and (unless asked) voice preserved.
- [ ] Significant changes explained.
- [ ] Measurably shorter/clearer than the original where that was the goal.

## Edge cases
- **Non-native author:** improve clarity without erasing their voice; don't over-standardize.
- **Legal/technical precision:** don't "simplify" defined terms or precise language.
- **Creative/marketing copy:** preserve intentional style; edit for impact, not just correctness.
- **Very rough draft:** may need drafting, not editing — flag if so.

## Examples
**Input:** "It is important to note that the reason for the delay in the project was
due to the fact that there were a number of unforeseen issues that arose during the
course of the implementation phase."
**Output:** "The project was delayed by several unforeseen issues during
implementation." — cut ~70% of the words; removed filler ("it is important to
note", "due to the fact that"); active and direct. Meaning preserved.

## Automation opportunities
- Offer tone/length variants (formal, brief) from one edit.
- Report a before/after word count and readability delta.
- Chain edit → proofread as a standard two-stage polish.
