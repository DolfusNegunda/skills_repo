## Choosing a summary type

| User asks for... | Type |
| --- | --- |
| "Summarize this", "key points", "what do I need to know" | Executive summary |
| "Risks", "obligations", "what are we on the hook for", "red flags" | Risk/obligation review |
| "What does it say about X", "find the payment terms", "pull out all dates" | Targeted extraction |
| Several documents: "compare", "combine", "what's the overall picture" | Multi-document synthesis |

Types combine freely — a contract review typically pairs an executive summary
with a risk/obligation review. Confirm the type and length before drafting when
the request is ambiguous.

## Executive summary

Audience: a decision-maker who will not read the document. Answer, in order:

1. **What is this?** — document type, parties, purpose, effective period.
2. **What matters most?** — money, deadlines, commitments, decisions required.
3. **What is unusual?** — anything off-market, one-sided, or unexpected.
4. **What happens next?** — actions, dates, or approvals the reader owns.

Structure: an opening sentence identifying the document, then 3-8 key points in
order of consequence (not document order), each with a source anchor. Close with
open questions or gaps if any exist.

## Risk/obligation review

For contracts, policies, and anything that binds a party. For each item capture:

- **Who** owes the duty (which party).
- **What** must be done or avoided.
- **When** — deadline, notice period, or trigger event.
- **Or else** — the consequence of failure (penalty, termination right, liability).
- **Where** — the clause or section reference.

Present as a table when there are more than three items. Always check for these
frequently-missed provisions and say explicitly whether each is present, absent,
or unclear:

- Auto-renewal and its opt-out notice window
- Liability caps and carve-outs from the cap
- Indemnities (who indemnifies whom, for what)
- Termination rights (for cause, for convenience, notice periods)
- Payment terms, late fees, price-increase mechanisms
- Confidentiality and data-handling duties that survive termination
- Assignment/change-of-control restrictions

Never characterize severity ("high risk") without stating the factual basis from
the text.

## Targeted extraction

Answering specific questions or pulling specific data from a document.

- Answer **only** from the document. General knowledge may explain a term, but
  must be labeled as background, not as document content.
- Quote or closely paraphrase the supporting passage and cite its location.
- If the document is silent: state "the document does not address this" — never
  substitute a typical or likely answer.
- If multiple passages bear on the question (e.g. a clause and a schedule that
  modifies it), cite all of them and reconcile or flag the conflict.
- For bulk extraction (all dates, all deliverables), sweep the entire document
  including annexes, and state the scope covered ("pp. 1-42, Schedules A-C").

## Multi-document synthesis

Combining or comparing several documents (e.g. a contract plus amendments, or
three vendor proposals).

1. Read and map each document individually first.
2. Establish precedence: amendments override originals; check order-of-precedence
   clauses; note effective dates.
3. Attribute every claim to its specific document ("MSA §3" vs "Amendment 2 §1").
4. Surface conflicts between documents explicitly rather than silently picking
   one reading.
5. For comparisons, use a shared dimension list (price, term, liability, exit)
   so gaps in one document are visible, and mark them "not addressed".

Never blend sources into an unattributed composite — the reader must be able to
trace each point to one document.

## Length controls: one-line / one-paragraph / one-page

Honor an explicit length exactly. When none is given, default to one page or
less and offer to expand or compress.

| Length | Size | Contains |
| --- | --- | --- |
| One-line | 1 sentence, ~25-40 words | Document identity + the single most consequential fact, with one anchor |
| One-paragraph | 3-6 sentences | Identity, purpose, 2-4 top facts, the biggest risk or action; anchors inline |
| One-page | ~300-500 words | Full executive summary structure; risk table if applicable; gaps noted |
| Full review | as needed | Everything above plus clause-by-clause detail on request |

Compression rule: shorter summaries drop detail, never accuracy or grounding —
even a one-liner carries at least one source anchor. When forced to cut, keep
money, deadlines, and liabilities; drop background and boilerplate first.

## Grounding conventions (citation anchors)

- Prefer the document's own reference system: `§4.2`, `Clause 12(b)`,
  `Schedule B`, `Section 3.1`.
- Fall back to page numbers (`p. 14`) or headings ("under 'Data Retention'")
  when the document lacks numbering.
- Direct quotes for anything contested, ambiguous, or high-stakes; paraphrase
  with an anchor for the rest.
- Label inferences: "the document implies X (based on §7 read with Schedule A)".
- Label legibility problems: "partially illegible on the scanned page 9; the
  visible text reads '…'".
- A claim with no possible anchor is removed, not kept.
