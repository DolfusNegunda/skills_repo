# Writing Executive Summaries

## Scope
Condensing a longer work into a short, standalone summary written for a decision-
maker: the conclusion first, the essential support, and the ask. Distinct from
neutral summarization ([summarizing-documents](../summarizing-documents/SKILL.md)),
which condenses without a decision frame.

## Purpose
Let a busy executive make or approve a decision from the summary alone — leading
with the answer, quantifying impact, and stating exactly what's being asked of them.

## When to use this skill
- "Write an executive summary / one-pager / board summary / TL;DR for leadership."
- The front page of a report, proposal, business case, or analysis.
- Compressing a long document specifically for a decision-maker.

## When NOT to use this skill
- Neutral, comprehensive summary → [summarizing-documents](../summarizing-documents/SKILL.md).
- The full document itself → [writing-reports](../writing-reports/SKILL.md) / [writing-proposals](../writing-proposals/SKILL.md).
- A short factual recap of a meeting → [summarizing-meeting-notes](../summarizing-meeting-notes/SKILL.md).

## Inputs
- The source document (or its findings) and the decision at stake.
- Who reads it and what they can approve/decide.
- The recommendation and its cost/benefit/risk.

## Outputs
- A one-page (or shorter) summary: the recommendation/conclusion up front, 3–5
  supporting points with numbers, key risks, and the specific ask.

## Workflow
```
Progress:
- [ ] 1. Identify the decision and who makes it
- [ ] 2. State the conclusion/recommendation in the first sentence
- [ ] 3. Give 3–5 supporting points, quantified
- [ ] 4. State impact, cost, and key risks honestly
- [ ] 5. State the specific ask (decision, budget, approval)
- [ ] 6. Ensure it stands alone; cut to one page
```

**Step 1 — Decision frame.** Know the decision and the decider. An exec summary
serves an action, not comprehension for its own sake.

**Step 2 — Answer first.** Sentence one is the conclusion/recommendation. Never make
the reader hunt for it. This is the single defining rule.

**Step 3 — Support, quantified.** 3–5 points that justify the conclusion, each with
a number where possible (impact, cost, timeline, risk). No narrative build-up.

**Step 4 — Honest impact & risk.** State the upside, the cost, and the top 1–3 risks
plainly. Hiding risk destroys trust and gets the summary sent back.

**Step 5 — The ask.** End with exactly what you need: approve X, fund $Y, decide by
Z. Ambiguity here wastes the whole summary.

**Step 6 — Standalone & short.** It must make sense without the full document, and
fit one page. If it needs the report to be understood, it isn't a summary.

## Principles
1. **Conclusion first.** The recommendation opens the document, not closes it.
2. **Quantify.** Numbers beat adjectives for decision-makers.
3. **Standalone.** Readable and actionable without the source.
4. **Honest about risk and cost.** Balanced summaries get trusted and approved.
5. **One page, ruthless.** Everything non-essential goes to the full document.

## Decision framework
- **Recommending action?** Recommendation → rationale → cost/risk → ask.
- **Reporting results?** Headline outcome → key drivers → implications → next step.
- **Multiple options?** State the recommended one first; note alternatives briefly.
- **Bad news?** Lead with it and the mitigation; don't bury it in paragraph three.

## Common mistakes
- **Chronological build-up** ("first we did X, then Y…") — conclusion arrives last.
- **Abstract-style teaser** that describes the document instead of stating findings.
- **No numbers** — vague claims a decision-maker can't act on.
- **Omitting risk/cost** — reads as a sales pitch, loses credibility.
- **No clear ask** — the reader doesn't know what decision to make.
- **Overlength** — two pages is not an executive summary.

## Validation checklist
- [ ] First sentence states the conclusion/recommendation.
- [ ] 3–5 supporting points, quantified where possible.
- [ ] Impact, cost, and top risks stated honestly.
- [ ] A specific ask (decision/approval/budget/date) is present.
- [ ] Reads and makes sense without the full document.
- [ ] Fits one page; no filler.
- [ ] Tone confident but balanced.

## Edge cases
- **Highly technical source:** translate to business impact; keep detail in the report.
- **Uncertain data:** state the confidence level; don't fake precision.
- **Politically sensitive:** stay factual and balanced; present options neutrally.
- **Very long source (100+ pages):** summarize the decision-relevant 10%, not everything.

## Examples
**Input:** "Summarize this 40-page vendor evaluation for the exec team."
**Output:** "Recommendation: select Vendor B and sign a 3-year contract by Q3.
Vendor B scored highest on security and TCO ($1.2M vs $1.6M over 3 years), meets all
must-haves, and can go live in 90 days. Key risk: their support SLA is weaker —
mitigated by a contractual credit clause. Ask: approve Vendor B and $1.2M budget at
the 12 July meeting." — followed by a 4-row comparison and the top three risks.

## Automation opportunities
- Generate a first-draft summary from a structured report, then refine the ask.
- Front every report/proposal template with this summary section by default.
- Produce audience variants (board vs. ops) from one source.
