# Crafting Presentation Narratives

## Scope
The thinking that must happen before slide design: the story, argument, key
messages, structure, and flow of a presentation. The narrative layer feeding
[building-powerpoint-decks](../building-powerpoint-decks/SKILL.md) and
[building-google-slides](../building-google-slides/SKILL.md).

## Purpose
Ensure a presentation has a spine — one clear message the audience follows and acts
on — so the slides that follow have something to carry, instead of being a pile of
information.

## When to use this skill
- "Structure my presentation" / "what should my deck say" / "help me tell this story."
- "My presentation doesn't land / feels like a list of facts."
- Preparing a pitch, keynote, board talk, or persuasive presentation.
- Before opening any slide tool.

## When NOT to use this skill
- Building the slides themselves → [building-powerpoint-decks](../building-powerpoint-decks/SKILL.md) / [building-google-slides](../building-google-slides/SKILL.md).
- A written document, not spoken → [writing-reports](../writing-reports/SKILL.md) / [writing-executive-summaries](../writing-executive-summaries/SKILL.md).
- Chart/visual design → [dataviz](../generating-data-reports/SKILL.md).

## Inputs
- The audience: who they are, what they care about, what they know, what they can decide.
- The one thing you want them to think, feel, or do afterward.
- The raw material (facts, data, story) available to support it.
- Time limit and setting.

## Outputs
- A narrative outline: the core message, the structure/arc, key points in sequence,
  the opening hook, and the closing ask — ready to turn into slides.

## Workflow
```
Progress:
- [ ] 1. Define the audience and the one desired outcome
- [ ] 2. Distill the single core message (the throughline)
- [ ] 3. Choose a narrative structure that fits the goal
- [ ] 4. Sequence key points; each earns its place
- [ ] 5. Design the opening (hook) and the close (ask)
- [ ] 6. Pressure-test the flow; then hand to slide design
```

**Step 1 — Audience & outcome.** Define who's listening and the single thing you want
them to do/think/feel. Everything serves that; without it, a talk becomes a data dump.

**Step 2 — One core message.** Distill to one sentence the audience should remember if
they forget everything else. If you can't, the presentation isn't ready. This
throughline is the spine.

**Step 3 — Structure to purpose.** Pick a fitting arc:
- **Situation–Complication–Resolution (SCR):** here's the world, here's the problem, here's the answer. Great for persuasion/pitches.
- **Problem–Solution–Benefit:** for proposals.
- **What–So what–Now what:** for updates/findings.
- **Chronological / journey:** for stories and case studies.
- **PREP (Point–Reason–Example–Point):** for short, punchy arguments.

**Step 4 — Sequence.** Order key points so each builds on the last toward the message.
Every point must advance the argument; cut anything that's merely interesting.

**Step 5 — Hook & ask.** Open with something that earns attention and frames why this
matters *to them* (a question, a surprising fact, a stakes-setting story) — not "agenda"
or "about us". Close with a specific, memorable ask/call to action.

**Step 6 — Pressure-test.** Read the outline as the audience: is the throughline
clear? Does each point follow? Is there a clear ask? Only then move to
[building-powerpoint-decks](../building-powerpoint-decks/SKILL.md) or
[building-google-slides](../building-google-slides/SKILL.md).

## Principles
1. **One core message.** A presentation is an argument, not an information dump.
2. **Audience-first.** Their needs, knowledge, and decision shape everything.
3. **Structure before slides.** The story exists before the deck.
4. **Every point advances the message,** or it's cut.
5. **Open with a hook, close with an ask.** Attention and action are earned.
6. **Story beats bullet lists** — people remember narrative, not data piles.

## Decision framework
- **Persuading/pitching?** SCR or Problem–Solution–Benefit.
- **Reporting results?** What–So what–Now what.
- **Teaching?** Progressive build, concept by concept, with examples.
- **Short slot (<10 min)?** One message, PREP, no meandering.
- **Skeptical audience?** Lead with their objection; earn agreement step by step.

## Common mistakes
- **No single message** — a tour of facts with no point.
- **Presenter-centric** ("about us", features) instead of audience needs.
- **Building slides first,** then reverse-engineering a story that isn't there.
- **Weak open** ("agenda", "thank you for having me") wasting the attention peak.
- **No ask** — the audience doesn't know what to do.
- **Too many points** — five ideas fight and none lands.

## Validation checklist
- [ ] Audience and the one desired outcome are defined.
- [ ] The core message is one clear sentence.
- [ ] A deliberate structure/arc fits the goal.
- [ ] Every key point advances the message; extras cut.
- [ ] The opening hooks and frames why-it-matters for this audience.
- [ ] The close states a specific ask/call to action.
- [ ] The outline holds up read from the audience's perspective.

## Edge cases
- **Hostile/skeptical audience:** open with their objection; build agreement incrementally.
- **Very technical content, non-technical audience:** lead with implications, not mechanism.
- **Data-heavy:** wrap the numbers in a story; the message is the point, data is support.
- **Very short slot:** one message, three points max, ruthless cutting.
- **Read-not-presented:** consider a document instead — narrative for speaking differs.

## Examples
**Input:** "I have to present our new platform to the board — help me structure it."
**Output:** Outcome: board approves phase-2 funding. Core message: "Our platform cut
onboarding time 60%; funding phase 2 unlocks $4M in new revenue." Structure (SCR):
current onboarding is our biggest churn driver (situation/complication) → the
platform's results so far (resolution) → what phase-2 funding delivers → the ask.
Hook: the $X we lose per month to slow onboarding. Close: approve $Y at this meeting.
Six beats, ready to build into ~10 slides.

## Automation opportunities
- Reuse proven narrative structures (SCR, PSB) as starting frames per talk type.
- Generate a first-draft outline from raw material, then refine the throughline.
- Hand the finished outline directly to a slide-building skill.
