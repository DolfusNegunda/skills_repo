# Writing Proposals

## Scope
Persuasive documents that seek a yes: proposals, SOWs, and RFP/tender responses.
Focus on client-centered structure, value articulation, and answering evaluation
criteria. Uses the executive-summary and business-prose skills as building blocks.

## Purpose
Win the work by making it easy for the buyer to choose you: their problem framed
first, a credible solution, clear value and pricing, and every stated evaluation
criterion explicitly addressed.

## When to use this skill
- "Write a proposal / SOW / bid / pitch document."
- "Respond to this RFP / tender / questionnaire."
- Any document persuading a client/stakeholder to approve work or spend.

## When NOT to use this skill
- Internal decision document → [writing-reports](../writing-reports/SKILL.md) / business case.
- Contract terms/legal drafting → legal review (proposals inform, don't replace, contracts).
- General persuasion memo → [writing-business-prose](../writing-business-prose/SKILL.md).

## Inputs
- The client's problem, goals, and (for RFPs) the exact requirements and evaluation
  criteria.
- Your solution, differentiators, relevant proof (cases, credentials), pricing, and timeline.
- Submission constraints (format, page limits, deadline, mandatory sections).

## Outputs
- A proposal/SOW structured around the client: executive summary, understanding of
  need, solution, value/ROI, deliverables & timeline, pricing, proof, and terms —
  with every RFP criterion explicitly answered.

## Workflow
```
Progress:
- [ ] 1. Analyze the client's problem and (for RFPs) every requirement + criterion
- [ ] 2. Lead with their need and desired outcome, not your company
- [ ] 3. Present the solution mapped to their problem
- [ ] 4. Quantify value / ROI; make the cost easy to justify
- [ ] 5. Give deliverables, timeline, and clear pricing
- [ ] 6. Add proof: relevant cases, credentials, risk mitigation
- [ ] 7. Address every evaluation criterion explicitly; check compliance
```

**Step 1 — Understand the buyer.** For RFPs, build a requirements matrix: every
requirement and evaluation criterion, mapped to where you answer it. Missing a
mandatory item often means automatic disqualification.

**Step 2 — Their problem first.** Open by demonstrating you understand *their*
situation and goals. Buyers reject proposals that lead with "We are a leading…".

**Step 3 — Solution mapped to need.** Present your approach as the answer to their
specific problem, feature→benefit→their-outcome. No generic capability dumps.

**Step 4 — Value & ROI.** Quantify the benefit (time saved, revenue, risk reduced)
so price is a small number next to a big one. Justify the investment.

**Step 5 — Deliverables, timeline, price.** Be specific and unambiguous about what
they get, when, and for how much. Vague scope loses trust and creates disputes.

**Step 6 — Proof & risk.** Relevant case studies, credentials, references, and how
you de-risk delivery. Address their likely objections before they raise them.

**Step 7 — Criteria & compliance.** Explicitly answer each evaluation criterion in
the buyer's language; confirm every mandatory section, format, and page limit is met.

## Principles
1. **Buyer-centered, not seller-centered.** Their problem and outcome lead.
2. **Answer the actual criteria,** in the buyer's words, explicitly.
3. **Quantify value** so price is easy to justify.
4. **Specific scope and price** — vagueness loses and creates disputes.
5. **Prove, don't claim.** Evidence beats adjectives.
6. **Comply exactly.** Format/page/mandatory-section failures disqualify.

## Decision framework
- **RFP with scored criteria?** Answer in their structure and language; build a compliance matrix.
- **Open proposal?** Lead with the problem and a compelling recommendation.
- **Price-sensitive buyer?** Emphasize ROI/TCO; offer tiered options.
- **Risk-averse buyer?** Emphasize proof, references, and risk mitigation.

## Common mistakes
- **Company-first opening** ("About us…") instead of the client's problem.
- **Ignoring evaluation criteria** or answering in your own framing, not theirs.
- **Generic, reused content** not tailored to this client.
- **Vague scope/pricing** — erodes trust and invites scope disputes.
- **Unsubstantiated claims** ("best-in-class") with no proof.
- **Non-compliance** with format, page limits, or mandatory sections.

## Validation checklist
- [ ] Opens with the client's problem and desired outcome.
- [ ] Solution maps directly to their stated needs.
- [ ] Value/ROI quantified.
- [ ] Deliverables, timeline, and pricing are specific and unambiguous.
- [ ] Relevant proof and risk mitigation included.
- [ ] Every RFP requirement and evaluation criterion explicitly addressed.
- [ ] Format, page limits, and mandatory sections comply exactly.
- [ ] Executive summary leads with the recommendation.

## Edge cases
- **Strict RFP templates:** follow their structure exactly, even if you'd organize differently.
- **Incumbent competitor:** emphasize differentiated value and switching benefit.
- **Unclear requirements:** state assumptions explicitly; ask clarifying questions if allowed.
- **Multi-party/consortium bids:** clarify roles, responsibilities, and single point of accountability.

## Examples
**Input:** "Respond to this RFP for a data-platform build; scored on approach,
experience, and price."
**Output:** Executive summary (their goal + our recommendation), understanding of
need, solution mapped to each requirement, a compliance matrix answering all 24
requirements, three relevant case studies, a phased timeline, fixed-price options
with ROI, and a risk-mitigation section — formatted to the RFP's mandated structure
and within the page limit.
