# Writing Business Prose

## Scope
The fundamentals of effective workplace writing — clarity, concision, structure,
and tone — applied to general business communication (memos, announcements,
briefings, updates). The foundation that genre skills (reports, proposals,
policies, email) build on.

## Purpose
Get the reader to understand and act with the least effort: the point comes first,
the language is plain, and every sentence earns its place.

## When to use this skill
- "Write this professionally / more clearly / more concisely."
- Drafting a memo, announcement, internal update, or briefing.
- Improving rambling, jargon-heavy, or buried-lede writing.
- As the base layer before applying a specific genre skill.

## When NOT to use this skill
- Email specifically → [drafting-business-email](../drafting-business-email/SKILL.md).
- A specific genre with its own structure → reports/proposals/policies/exec-summary skills.
- Surface correctness only → [proofreading-text](../proofreading-text/SKILL.md).
- Restructuring an existing draft → [editing-prose](../editing-prose/SKILL.md).

## Inputs
- The message and its purpose (inform, request, persuade, decide).
- Audience (seniority, expertise, relationship) and desired tone.
- Any length constraint and the single action you want from the reader.

## Outputs
- Clear business prose that states the point first, uses plain language, and ends
  with a specific ask or next step.

## Workflow
```
Progress:
- [ ] 1. Define purpose, audience, and the one action wanted
- [ ] 2. State the main point first (BLUF)
- [ ] 3. Structure supporting content logically
- [ ] 4. Write plainly; cut every unneeded word
- [ ] 5. Set tone to match audience and relationship
- [ ] 6. End with a clear ask / next step
- [ ] 7. Proofread
```

**Step 1 — Purpose & audience.** Know what you want the reader to *do* and who they
are. Writing without a target action rambles.

**Step 2 — Point first (BLUF).** Lead with the bottom line — the decision, request,
or key fact. Busy readers should get it from the first two sentences.

**Step 3 — Structure.** Group supporting points logically (by importance, sequence,
or theme). Use short paragraphs, headings for longer pieces, and lists for parallel
items.

**Step 4 — Plain language.** Prefer short words, active voice, and concrete nouns.
Cut filler ("in order to"→"to"), hedges, nominalizations ("make a decision"→
"decide"), and jargon the reader won't share. One idea per sentence.

**Step 5 — Tone.** Professional and warm by default; more formal upward/external,
more direct with peers. Never condescending or passive-aggressive.

**Step 6 — Close with the ask.** End with the specific next step, owner, and date.
Vague endings produce no action.

**Step 7 — Proofread.** Finish with [proofreading-text](../proofreading-text/SKILL.md).

## Principles
1. **Point first.** Bottom line up front; never bury the lede.
2. **Plain over impressive.** Clarity is the goal, not vocabulary.
3. **Concise.** If a word can go, cut it. Respect the reader's time.
4. **Active and concrete.** "The team shipped it," not "it was shipped".
5. **Reader-focused.** Write for what they need to know and do.

## Decision framework
- **Informing?** Lead with the fact; give only context they need.
- **Requesting?** Lead with the ask; then the why; then the how/when.
- **Persuading?** Lead with the recommendation; support with evidence.
- **Sensitive?** Direct but empathetic; state the situation, impact, and path forward.
- **Long?** Add an executive summary → [writing-executive-summaries](../writing-executive-summaries/SKILL.md).

## Common mistakes
- **Burying the point** under context and preamble.
- **Passive voice and nominalizations** that hide who does what.
- **Jargon and acronyms** the reader doesn't share.
- **Wall of text** with no headings, lists, or paragraph breaks.
- **No clear ask** — the reader doesn't know what to do next.
- **Padding** ("it should be noted that", "at this point in time").

## Validation checklist
- [ ] The main point is in the first two sentences.
- [ ] The reader knows exactly what action to take, by when.
- [ ] Active voice dominates; sentences are one idea each.
- [ ] No unexplained jargon/acronyms for this audience.
- [ ] Every sentence earns its place; filler removed.
- [ ] Tone matches audience and relationship.
- [ ] Formatting (headings/lists) aids scanning where length warrants.

## Edge cases
- **Bad news:** be direct and humane; don't bury it, don't soften into vagueness.
- **Cross-cultural/global readers:** simpler syntax, no idioms, define acronyms.
- **Highly technical → non-technical:** translate, don't dumb down; lead with implications.
- **Legal/regulated content:** precision beats brevity; pair with the policy skill.

## Examples
**Input:** "We regret to inform all staff that due to a variety of factors the
office relocation which was previously planned will now be taking place at a later
date to be confirmed."
**Output:** "The office move is postponed. We'll confirm the new date by 15 August.
Nothing you need to do now — current arrangements continue until then."

## Automation opportunities
- Standardize memo/announcement templates for recurring comms.
- Pair with a tone/length variant pass for audience-specific versions.
- Use as the base pass before any genre skill formats the final artifact.
