# Reviewing Presentations

## Scope
Evaluation of decks and talks for whether they land with their audience — narrative,
message, and slide craft. Inherits method/severity/scoring from
[conducting-structured-reviews](../conducting-structured-reviews/SKILL.md). Building
decks is [building-powerpoint-decks](../../office/building-powerpoint-decks/SKILL.md).

## Purpose
Tell the presenter whether the deck makes its case and holds attention, and exactly
what to change — judging the story before the styling.

## When to use this skill
- "Review my deck / presentation / slides / pitch."
- A dry-run gate before presenting to execs, a board, or a client.
- Diagnosing why a presentation "isn't landing".

## When NOT to use this skill
- Build/redesign the deck → [building-powerpoint-decks](../../office/building-powerpoint-decks/SKILL.md) / [building-google-slides](../../office/building-google-slides/SKILL.md).
- Structure the story from scratch → [crafting-presentation-narratives](../../office/crafting-presentation-narratives/SKILL.md).
- Chart-only critique → [reviewing-dashboards](../reviewing-dashboards/SKILL.md).

## Inputs
- The deck (and speaker notes if available), the audience, setting, and time limit.
- The one outcome the presenter wants (decide, fund, understand, buy).

## Outputs
- A review: verdict + scores, severity-ranked findings (slide # + why + fix), and
  strengths.

## Evaluation rubric (dimensions)
1. **Narrative** — one clear throughline; logical arc; each slide advances it.
2. **Message clarity** — takeaway titles (not topics); the ask is explicit.
3. **Audience fit** — right level, right framing, addresses their concerns.
4. **Slide design** — one idea per slide, legible, consistent, uncluttered.
5. **Evidence & visuals** — charts make one honest point; claims supported.
6. **Flow & length** — opening hook, strong close, fits the time (~1 slide/min).

## Scoring & severity
Score 1–5; rank Blocker→Praise (see
[foundation reference](../conducting-structured-reviews/references/severity-and-scoring.md)).
Examples: **Blocker** = no clear ask/message — the audience won't know what to do;
**Major** = the recommendation is buried on slide 20; **Minor** = a wall-of-text
slide; **Nit** = inconsistent fonts.

## Workflow
```
Progress:
- [ ] 1. Confirm audience, setting, time, and desired outcome
- [ ] 2. Assess the narrative from titles alone (do they tell the story?)
- [ ] 3. Check message clarity and the ask
- [ ] 4. Review slide design, evidence, and length
- [ ] 5. Severity-rank, score, verdict; fixes per finding
```

**Step 2 — the title test.** Read only the slide titles in order: if they don't
convey the argument, the narrative is broken — the highest-leverage finding.
**Prioritize story over styling** — a beautiful deck with no point still fails.

## Recommended-improvements guidance
Fixes by dimension: rewrite a topic title into a takeaway; move the ask to the
front; cut a text-heavy slide to one point; replace a chart that hides its message;
trim slides to fit the time.

## Validation checklist
- [ ] Audience, setting, and desired outcome known.
- [ ] Narrative assessed via the title-only test.
- [ ] The core message and the ask are explicit.
- [ ] Slide design, evidence honesty, and length checked.
- [ ] Findings carry slide #, severity, and a fix; verdict + scores given.
- [ ] Strengths noted; story issues ranked above styling.

## Common mistakes
- **Styling feedback while the deck has no throughline.**
- **Ignoring the audience** — judging by the reviewer's taste.
- **Missing the absent ask.**
- **Not using speaker notes/context** to judge live vs. read.

## Edge cases
- **Sent-not-presented deck:** it must stand alone; judge self-sufficiency, not just talk support.
- **Board/exec:** the ask and recommendation up front is a Blocker if missing.
- **Technical audience:** depth expected; over-simplification is a finding.

## Examples
**Input:** "Review my 15-slide board deck before Thursday."
**Output:** Verdict: Approve-with-changes (Narrative 3/5, Message 2/5). **Blocker:**
no explicit ask — add a "Decision requested" slide up front. **Major:** titles are
topics ("Q3 Results") not takeaways; rewrite as claims. **Minor:** slide 9 is a wall
of text. **Praise:** strong opening hook.

## Automation opportunities
- Run the title-only test as a quick standing check on any deck.
- Pair with a narrative skill to fix throughline issues found.
