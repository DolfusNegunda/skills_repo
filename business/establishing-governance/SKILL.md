# Establishing Governance

## Scope
Designing how decisions get made and who's accountable — decision rights, roles,
forums (boards/committees), escalation, and cadence — for a project, program, team,
or domain. Right-sized, not bureaucratic.

## Purpose
Make decision-making clean, fast, and consistent: everyone knows who decides what,
how issues escalate, and who's accountable — eliminating the stalls and re-litigation
that come from unclear authority.

## When to use this skill
- "Set up governance / define decision rights / who decides what."
- "Create a steering committee / operating model / decision framework."
- Fixing slow, unclear, or re-litigated decision-making.

## When NOT to use this skill
- Organizational policy documents → [../office/writing-policies](../../office/writing-policies/SKILL.md).
- Project scheduling → [planning-projects](../planning-projects/SKILL.md).
- Stakeholder engagement → [analyzing-stakeholders](../analyzing-stakeholders/SKILL.md).

## Inputs
- The scope to govern and the types/frequency of decisions it involves.
- The people/roles, current pain (bottlenecks, unclear authority), and risk/compliance needs.

## Outputs
- A governance model: decision rights (RACI/RAPID), roles and forums with mandates,
  escalation paths, meeting cadence, and accountability — documented concisely.

## Workflow
```
Progress:
- [ ] 1. Identify the decisions that need governing and their frequency
- [ ] 2. Assign decision rights (who decides, who's consulted/informed)
- [ ] 3. Define roles and forums with clear mandates
- [ ] 4. Set escalation paths and thresholds
- [ ] 5. Right-size cadence and reporting
- [ ] 6. Document accountability; review effectiveness
```

**Step 1 — govern decisions, not activity.** Start from the actual decisions (spend
approval, scope change, prioritization) — governance exists to make these cleanly.
**Step 2 — one decider per decision** (RAPID's "D" / RACI's "A"); shared decision
rights cause stalls. **Step 3 — every forum needs a mandate** (what it decides) or it
becomes a status meeting. **Step 5 — right-size:** enough governance to control risk,
not so much it slows everything. Over-governance is as damaging as none.

## Principles
1. **Govern decisions,** not activity or attendance.
2. **One accountable decider** per decision type.
3. **Forums have mandates,** not just meetings.
4. **Clear escalation** with thresholds — no decision stuck with nowhere to go.
5. **Right-size to risk** — proportionate, not bureaucratic.
6. **Transparent and documented** — authority everyone can see.

## Decision framework
- **High-risk/high-value decisions?** Formal board/committee with defined mandate.
- **Frequent operational decisions?** Delegate with clear thresholds; escalate exceptions.
- **Cross-functional conflict?** A forum with authority to arbitrate, not just discuss.
- **Small team?** Lightweight RACI, not a committee structure.

## Common mistakes
- **Unclear decision rights** — decisions stall or get re-litigated.
- **Committees without mandates** — status theatre, no decisions.
- **Over-governance** — approvals for trivial things; velocity dies.
- **No escalation path** — issues stuck at the wrong level.
- **Governing activity** (attendance, updates) instead of decisions.
- **Shared accountability** — everyone responsible, no one decides.

## Validation checklist
- [ ] The decisions to be governed are identified.
- [ ] Each decision type has one accountable decider (RACI/RAPID).
- [ ] Forums have explicit mandates and membership.
- [ ] Escalation paths and thresholds defined.
- [ ] Cadence and reporting are proportionate to risk.
- [ ] Model documented and transparent; a review mechanism exists.

## Edge cases
- **Regulated domains:** align governance with mandated controls and audit needs.
- **Matrixed orgs:** clarify authority across the functional/project axes to avoid deadlock.
- **Startups/scale-ups:** start light; add governance as complexity grows, not before.
- **Program vs. project:** program governance sets direction; project governance executes — keep the layers distinct.

## Examples
**Input:** "Set up governance for our multi-team platform program — decisions keep stalling."
**Output:** Decision inventory (architecture, prioritization, spend); a RAPID map
giving architecture decisions to the lead architect (D) with tech leads consulted;
a fortnightly steering forum with a mandate over prioritization and spend up to
$50k (above escalates to the sponsor); clear escalation thresholds; a lightweight
weekly delivery sync for operational calls — documented on one page.

## Automation opportunities
- Maintain a decision log tied to the governance model for traceability.
- Route approvals automatically by threshold to the right decider.
