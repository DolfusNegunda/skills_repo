# Communicating with Stakeholders

## Scope
Planning and producing communications that land with each stakeholder group —
tailoring content, channel, cadence, and framing to their needs and influence. Turns
[analyzing-stakeholders](../analyzing-stakeholders/SKILL.md) into a concrete plan and
messages. General writing craft is
[writing-business-prose](../../office/writing-business-prose/SKILL.md).

## Purpose
Ensure the right people get the right message in the right way at the right time — so
stakeholders stay informed, aligned, and supportive, rather than surprised,
over-messaged, or told the wrong thing for their needs.

## When to use this skill
- "Create a communication plan / stakeholder comms plan."
- "How do I communicate this change/decision/status to X?"
- Managing messaging for a project, change, or announcement.

## When NOT to use this skill
- The stakeholder analysis itself → [analyzing-stakeholders](../analyzing-stakeholders/SKILL.md).
- General writing/tone → [writing-business-prose](../../office/writing-business-prose/SKILL.md) / [drafting-business-email](../../office/drafting-business-email/SKILL.md).
- The change program → [managing-change](../managing-change/SKILL.md).

## Inputs
- The stakeholder analysis (who, power/interest, attitude, needs).
- What you need to communicate and the outcome you want (inform, align, secure support).

## Outputs
- A communication plan (audience × message × channel × frequency × owner) plus
  tailored key messages/drafts per group.

## Workflow
```
Progress:
- [ ] 1. Start from the stakeholder analysis (who + needs + influence)
- [ ] 2. Define the objective per audience (inform/align/support)
- [ ] 3. Tailor the message and framing to each group's interests
- [ ] 4. Choose channel and frequency per group
- [ ] 5. Assign owners and timing; plan two-way feedback
- [ ] 6. Draft key messages; keep them consistent at the core
```

**Step 3 — tailor framing, not facts.** The same truth is framed for what each
audience cares about (execs: impact and risk; teams: what changes for them;
customers: benefit). One broadcast for everyone serves no one well. **Step 4 —
channel and cadence by group:** high-power/high-interest get direct, frequent,
two-way contact; others get lighter touch. **Step 6 — consistent core:** tailoring
must not become contradiction — the underlying message stays the same across
audiences, or trust erodes when they compare notes.

## Principles
1. **Audience-tailored, single-core.** Frame per group; never contradict across them.
2. **Effort follows influence** — engagement matches power/interest.
3. **Right channel + right cadence,** not one email to all.
4. **Two-way,** not just broadcast — plan for feedback.
5. **Timely and proactive** — silence breeds rumor.
6. **Lead with what they care about,** not what you want to say.

## Decision framework
- **High power/high interest?** Direct, frequent, two-way (briefings, 1:1s).
- **High power/low interest?** Concise, periodic, decision-relevant only.
- **Low power/high interest?** Regular updates; recruit as advocates.
- **Bad news?** Direct, fast, and to the affected first — before it leaks.
- **Complex/sensitive?** Live/synchronous over written; allow questions.

## Common mistakes
- **One message for all audiences** — irrelevant to most.
- **Same effort to everyone** — under-serving the critical few.
- **Broadcast-only** — no feedback loop.
- **Wrong channel** (a major change buried in a mass email).
- **Too little, too late** — rumor fills the gap.
- **Inconsistent core message** across groups — erodes trust.

## Validation checklist
- [ ] Built from the stakeholder analysis (needs + influence).
- [ ] Per-audience objective defined.
- [ ] Message framed to each group; core stays consistent.
- [ ] Channel and frequency set per group.
- [ ] Owners, timing, and a feedback mechanism in place.
- [ ] Key messages drafted; leads with audience interest.

## Edge cases
- **Bad news/crisis:** speed, honesty, and sequence (affected first) matter most.
- **Confidential/phased info:** plan who learns what when; avoid leaks and inequity.
- **Global/distributed:** time zones, languages, and local context.
- **Message discipline:** for sensitive topics, agree talking points so spokespeople stay consistent.

## Examples
**Input:** "Plan comms for a reorg affecting three teams."
**Output:** Plan built from the stakeholder map: leadership briefed first (1:1s, full
detail); affected managers next (briefing + FAQ + talking points) so they can lead
their teams; teams via manager-led sessions (what changes for you) then a written
recap; a two-way channel for questions; consistent core message across all, framed
per audience, sequenced to reach affected people before any broad announcement.

## Automation opportunities
- Generate audience variants from one core message.
- Track the comms plan (audience × message × date × owner × status) as a living table.
